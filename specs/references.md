# Scientific References

These references must appear in the footer of the generated PDF and are cited throughout the modules.

## Bibliography

1. **Bravo-Jaico, J. et al. (2025)**. Model for assessing the maturity level of digital transformation in higher education institutions. *Frontiers in Education*.
   - Used in: Module 1 (MTM Model, 8 dimensions, 3 sub-criteria per dimension, 3 maturity levels)
   - Key insight: The #1 barrier is not technology but lack of personnel preparation. Invest in digital culture and soft skills.

2. **Bui, H. Q. et al. (2025)**. AI adoption: a new perspective from accounting students in Vietnam. *Journal of Asian Business and Economic Studies*.
   - Used in: Module 4 (social influence works for students, peer-driven adoption, facilitating conditions)
   - Key insight: For students, social influence and success stories are powerful drivers. Partner with industry.

3. **Cao, K. et al. (2026)**. AI anxiety and adoption intention in higher education based on an extended TAM-UTAUT. *Scientific Reports*.
   - Used in: Module 2 (3 anxiety types), Module 4 (champion warning for professors)
   - Key insight: Displacement anxiety blocks all adoption. Sociotechnical anxiety CAN be leveraged positively. STEM profiles adapt faster than humanities.

4. **Deacon, B. et al. (2025)**. Resisting digital change at the university: an exploration into triggers and organisational countermeasures. *European Journal of Higher Education*.
   - Used in: Module 2 (4 resistance behaviors: pronounced refusal/opposing, subtle undermining/avoiding)
   - Key insight: Resistance is a diagnostic tool, not an obstacle. Workload overload is the #1 barrier. Offer tangible rewards (reduced teaching load, financial incentives). Co-create, don't impose top-down.

5. **Hong, T. T. M. et al. (2026)**. Discovering acceptance and intention to use artificial intelligence for learning among pre-service teachers in Vietnam. *Discover Education*.
   - Used in: Module 2 (ethical barriers as an asset), Module 4 (faculty training recommendations)
   - Key insight: Perceived barriers (ethics, cheating, over-reliance) reflect critical engagement, not opposition. Use these people to draft AI ethical guidelines. Faculty must provide formal frameworks — without them, peer mimicry leads to unethical use.

6. **Nguyen, H. L., & Hong, Y. (2025)**. National policy analysis of digital transformation in Vietnamese higher education. *Policy Futures in Education*.
   - Used in: Module 4 (3-layer deployment model: Activation/Implementation/Institutionalization)
   - Key insight: Structure change in 3 strict phases (Lewin-based). Address the digital divide (urban/rural). Students are consultants, not guinea pigs. "Refreezing" must be dynamic — continuous adaptation.

7. **Nguyen, N. D., & Uong, L. N. T. (2026)**. Digital transformation in non-public universities: empirical evidence from Hanoi City. *Cogent Social Sciences*.
   - Used in: Context (empirical evidence from Vietnamese HE sector)

8. **Singh, H., & Strzelecki, A. (2026)**. Adoption of generative AI by academics. (Springer, referenced in programme)
   - Used in: Module 2 (3 diffusion levers: relative advantage, compatibility, low complexity), Module 4 (professor-specific strategy)
   - Key insight: For professors, do NOT use social pressure or public demos. They evaluate in private. Offer risk-free solo experimentation and demonstrate immediate time savings on THEIR tasks.
