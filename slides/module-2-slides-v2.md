### SLIDE 2 — The 3 AI Anxieties
*Cao et al. (2026) — Scientific Reports*

Not all resistance is the same. Research identifies **3 distinct anxieties** specific to AI adoption:

| Anxiety | What they feel | What they say |
|---|---|---|
| **AI Learning Anxiety** | "I can't master this technology" | "I'm not technical enough" |
| **Sociotechnical Blindness** | "The institution will leave me behind" | "Nobody told me about this" |
| **Job Displacement Anxiety** | "AI will replace me" | "What's the point of my expertise now?" |

**Displacement anxiety blocks ALL adoption** — if someone believes AI will make them irrelevant, no amount of training will help. You must address the existential fear first.

But sociotechnical anxiety **can be leveraged positively** — it creates urgency to learn. Channel it into a community of practice.

### SLIDE 3 — The Special Case: Ethical Engagement Is an ASSET
*Hong et al. (2026) — Discover Education*

Some people oppose AI because they ask hard questions:
- "What about academic integrity?"
- "Students will just copy-paste"
- "Who is responsible when the AI is wrong?"

**This is NOT resistance. This is critical thinking.**

These people are your most valuable allies for:
- Drafting AI ethics guidelines
- Creating student AI conduct codes
- Building institutional frameworks for responsible use

Without formal frameworks, students learn AI from peers — and **peer mimicry leads to unethical use** (Hong et al., 2026). You NEED these critical voices.

### SLIDE 4 — Public vs. Private Universities: Different Blockers
*Verano-Tacoronte et al. (2025) — Education and Information Technologies*

**Important nuance**: Cao's framework assumes displacement anxiety is the main blocker. But when job security is not a concern (public universities, tenured faculty), **the real blockers are different**:

| Cao's framework (private/unstable) | Verano-Tacoronte's finding (public/stable) |
|---|---|
| "AI will take my job" | "AI will make me teach badly" |
| Displacement anxiety | Ethical & pedagogical anxiety |
| Address: career reassurance | Address: ethical frameworks, student conduct codes |

**Two surprising findings:**
- **Gender matters**: female faculty show lower AI adoption intention → design targeted mentoring
- **Age does NOT matter**: don't assume older = more resistant. That's a myth.

**Even faculty who refuse to use AI must be trained** — because their students are already using it.

### SLIDE 5 — The Discipline Factor
*Cao et al. (2026)*

**Not all faculty react the same way to AI anxiety:**

| Discipline | Reaction | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **STEM** | Convert anxiety into motivation more easily | Show immediate research utility: data analysis, literature review, code generation |
| **Humanities & Social Sciences** | Perceive AI as a threat to their disciplinary identity | Position AI as a critical analysis tool, NOT a replacement for intellectual work |

Your strategy for the Engineering department ≠ your strategy for the Literature department.

### SLIDE 6 — The 3 Missing Adoption Levers
*Singh & Strzelecki (2026) — Springer*

When someone doesn't adopt AI, one of these 3 rational levers is missing:

| Missing Lever | They think... | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Relative Advantage** | "I don't see how AI improves MY work" | Show time savings on THEIR specific tasks. Not generic demos. |
| **Compatibility** | "It doesn't fit my workflow" | Adapt the tool to THEIR methods. Don't ask them to change for the tool. |
| **Low Complexity** | "It's too complicated" | No installation, no login, one-click access, guided tutorials. |

Study of 640 academics across 10 universities. These 3 levers explain most of the variance in adoption.

### SLIDE 7 — The Professor Exception
*Singh & Strzelecki (2026)*

> "For professors: do NOT rely on social pressure or public demonstrations."

Academics evaluate tools **privately**, in **isolation**.

| What does NOT work | What WORKS |
|---|---|
| "Your colleague is already using it!" | Risk-free, private experimentation |
| Public demos with an audience | No installation, no sign-up, just try |
| The "champion" model with peer pressure | Immediate time savings on THEIR tasks |
| Top-down mandates | No audience, no judgment |

Social influence works for students (Bui et al., 2025). It **backfires** with professors.

### SLIDE 8 — How the Triple Diagnostic Generates a Strategy

In the app, you select 3 things for each stakeholder:

1. **Behavior**: How do they resist? (supportive / refusing / opposing / undermining / avoiding)
2. **Anxiety**: Why do they resist? (learning / sociotechnical / displacement / ethical engagement)
3. **Missing Lever**: What rational argument is missing? (advantage / compatibility / complexity)

The platform combines all 3 to generate a **personalized counter-measure**.

**Example**: Professor — Subtle Avoiding — Learning Anxiety — Low Complexity missing

> *"This professor avoids AI because it feels overwhelming, not because they oppose it. Provide a private sandbox. Start with a tool that augments their existing research workflow. No public demos, no champion pressure."*

### SLIDE 9 — Power/Interest: Prioritizing Your Effort

You mapped stakeholders by **Power** and **Interest** this morning. In the app, these axes combine with the triple diagnostic to tell you **where to focus your energy**:

| | High Interest | Low Interest |
|---|---|---|
| **High Power** | **KEY PLAYER** — Your most intense engagement. Direct meetings, co-design. | **KEEP SATISFIED** — Dangerous if ignored. Inform, no surprises. |
| **Low Power** | **KEEP INFORMED** — Your early adopters and pilots. Leverage their energy. | **MONITOR** — Minimal effort. Brief updates only. |

The app shows a **color-coded badge** on each stakeholder card and adds a **priority recommendation** to the counter-measure.

A Key Player with displacement anxiety ≠ the same response as a Monitor with learning anxiety. **The combination is what matters.**
