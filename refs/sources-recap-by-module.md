## Module 1 — Maturity Diagnostic (Day 1)

### Bravo-Jaico, J. et al. (2025)
**Model for assessing the maturity level of digital transformation in higher education institutions**
*Frontiers in Education* | [Full text](https://doi.org/10.3389/feduc.2025.1581648)

- Provides the **MTM framework** with **8 dimensions** (socio-cultural, teaching & learning, academic management, admin management, research & innovation, digital governance, institutional image, university extension)
- Each dimension is evaluated across **3 sub-criteria**: Tools & Processes, Data, Culture
- **3 maturity levels**: Beginner, In Progress, Continuous Improvement
- Key finding: **the #1 barrier is lack of personnel preparation**, not technology
- Recommends investing in digital culture and soft skills, not just infrastructure
- Warns against "isolated gadget syndrome" — tech must align with the institution's strategic plan

## Module 2 — Resistance Mapping (Day 2)

### Deacon, B. et al. (2025)
**Resisting digital change at the university: an exploration into triggers and organisational countermeasures**
*European Journal of Higher Education* | [Full text](https://doi.org/10.1080/21568235.2025.2512735)

- Study of 68 staff from 8 European universities
- **4 resistance behaviors** taxonomy:
  - *Pronounced Refusal* — open opposition, vocal rejection
  - *Pronounced Opposing* — active argumentation, ideological pushback
  - *Subtle Undermining* — passive sabotage, quiet non-compliance (most dangerous)
  - *Subtle Avoiding* — minimization, deflection, "it's just a fad"
- **Workload overload** is the #1 trigger for resistance
- Resistance is a **diagnostic tool**, not an obstacle — it reveals real tensions
- Recommends: tangible rewards (reduced teaching load, financial incentives), co-creation over top-down mandates

### Cao, K. et al. (2026)
**AI anxiety and adoption intention in higher education based on an extended TAM-UTAUT**
*Scientific Reports* | [Full text](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-026-35823-9)

- **3 types of AI anxiety**:
  - *AI Learning Anxiety* — fear of not mastering the technology
  - *Sociotechnical Blindness* — fear of being marginalized by the institution
  - *Job Displacement Anxiety* — fear of losing professional value and identity
- **Displacement anxiety blocks ALL adoption** — must be addressed first
- Sociotechnical anxiety **can be leveraged positively** to motivate learning
- **STEM profiles adapt faster** than humanities — different strategies needed per discipline
- Self-efficacy is the key mediator: hands-on workshops with 1-to-1 tutoring build confidence

### Hong, T. T. M. et al. (2026)
**Discovering acceptance and intention to use AI for learning among pre-service teachers in Vietnam**
*Discover Education (Springer)* | [Full text](https://doi.org/10.1007/s44217-026-01289-0)

- Perceived barriers (ethics, cheating, over-reliance) reflect **critical engagement, not opposition**
- These people are **assets** — use them to draft AI ethical guidelines
- Without formal faculty frameworks, **peer mimicry leads to unethical use**
- Faculty must provide formal pedagogical frameworks for responsible AI use

### Singh, H. & Strzelecki, A. (2026)
**Academics as adopters of generative AI: an application of diffusion of innovations theory**
*Education and Information Technologies (Springer)* | [Full text](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10639-025-13835-8)

- Study of 640 academics across 10 universities
- **3 adoption levers** (from diffusion of innovations):
  - *Relative Advantage* — "How does this improve MY work?"
  - *Compatibility* — "Does it fit MY existing workflow?"
  - *Low Complexity* — "Is it easy enough for me to try?"
- **Critical insight for professors**: do NOT use social pressure or public demos — they evaluate tools **in private**
- Strategy: offer **risk-free solo experimentation** and demonstrate immediate time savings on their specific tasks

## Module 3 — Solutions Arsenal (Day 3)

No dedicated research article. Module 3 is the **practical vibe-coding workshop** where participants build a quick win prototype.

Indirectly informed by:
- **Singh & Strzelecki (2026)** — the prototype must demonstrate "relative advantage" to skeptical professors
- **Deacon et al. (2025)** — link the quick win to a resistance trigger identified in Module 2
- **Bravo-Jaico et al. (2025)** — the solution should address the weakest MTM dimension from Module 1

## Module 4 — 90-Day Plan (Day 4)

### Nguyen, H. L. & Hong, Y. (2025)
**National policy analysis of digital transformation in Vietnamese higher education: Conceptualising a three-layer model for implementation**
*Policy Futures in Education* | [Full text](https://doi.org/10.1177/14782103251348089)

- **3-layer deployment model** (Lewin-based):
  - **Activation** (Days 1-30) — infrastructure, vision, equity, budget
  - **Implementation** (Days 31-60) — pedagogy, admin tools, faculty training
  - **Institutionalization** (Days 61-90) — KPIs, quality assurance, stakeholder engagement
- **Digital divide warning**: urban/rural gap in Vietnam — plans must include equity measures
- Students are **consultants, not guinea pigs** — include student feedback loops
- "Refreezing" must be **dynamic** — continuous adaptation, not a frozen state

### Bui, H. Q. et al. (2025)
**AI adoption: a new perspective from accounting students in Vietnam**
*Journal of Asian Business and Economic Studies* | [Full text](https://doi.org/10.1108/JABES-06-2024-0300)

- For **students**, social influence and success stories are powerful adoption drivers
- Facilitating conditions matter: accessible tech support, AI courses in curricula
- Recommends **industry partnerships** to align training with market needs

### Cao, K. et al. (2026) *(also used in Module 2)*
- Module 4 uses this for **champion assignment warnings**: if a stakeholder has displacement anxiety, do NOT assign them as champion — it will backfire

### Singh, H. & Strzelecki, A. (2026) *(also used in Module 2)*
- Module 4 uses this for **professor-specific strategy**: if a professor is mapped as a stakeholder, avoid the public champion model — offer private experimentation instead

### Hong, T. T. M. et al. (2026) *(also used in Module 2)*
- Module 4 uses this for **faculty training recommendations**: formal frameworks are necessary to prevent unethical peer mimicry

## Cross-cutting (Context)

### Nguyen, N. D. & Uong, L. N. T. (2026)
**Digital transformation in non-public universities: empirical evidence from Hanoi City**
*Cogent Social Sciences* | [Full text](https://doi.org/10.1080/23311886.2026.2632388)

- Empirical evidence from Vietnamese non-public HE sector
- External pressure is a significant driver of digital transformation
- Provides **contextual grounding** for the Vietnamese landscape addressed throughout the seminar
- Not directly used in any single module but informs the overall workshop framing
